Why I Want to Join Automattic

About a year ago, I decided that I needed a career-change and embarked on a long and interesting journey that included making it to the final interviews for the U.S. Foreign Service, earning my PMP certification, and interviewing a diverse set of industry leaders. After researching Automattic extensively and speaking with Aaron Hohle at length, I knew that I wanted to be an Automattician. Your organizational culture and your employees' warmth, dedication, and competence are inspiring and the TAM position offers unique and exciting opportunities in both the responsibilities and clientele.

The Organization

Automattic's culture, rooted in transparency and integrity, and vision, seeking to extend publishing and commerce tools globally, inspire tens of thousands of people around the world to volunteer to help the movement. In addition, you make people around the world feel empowered in their work and provide them with the autonomy to pursue interesting problems. These attributes alone set Automattic apart from most other technology companies of comparable success and have convinced me that, by working for you, I would be able to make a tangible and positive impact on the world.

The People

As may not surprise you, both your employees and volunteers are prolific and candid in their descriptions of life as an Automattician. Much of what I have learned has come from the WordPress slack instance, community forums, and individual blogs. I've also thoroughly enjoyed Matt's podcast, Distributed, and learning about every facet of distributed work. What is immediately clear is that Automattic honors and empowers its people and the community members care deeply about each other. Who wouldn't want to be a part of that?

The Position

On top of joining a great organization and getting to work with intelligent, driven, and empathetic colleagues, the TechnicalAccount Manager position offers an exciting set of clients and responsibilities. I thrive under pressure and purpose and would \relish the opportunity to collaborate with business leaders around the world on complex, interesting problems make me vibrate with excitement. It's also clear that work will never be linear or stale as every debug, expanded functionality, and site redesign is unique and brings with the opportunity to learn and grow. That combination: incredible clients, engaging problems, and continual learning, is exactly what I want in a career.

Why I Am a Good Fit

At first glance, my experience in software development, geology, and academic research suggests that I am primarily a researcher and engineer (not a bad thing!). While my strong technical background is undeniable, it does not define me. For the past 14 years, I've thrived in a diverse set of roles that have provided me with extensive management experience, honed my interpersonal skills, and made me an amiable and reliable colleague. Automattic's combination of highly collaborative work and a globally dispersed workforce makes these skills essential tools for every employee.

Project Management

Fresh out of highschool, I started as the manager of a research lab at UC San Diego, where I built a student worker program and hired, trained, and supervised a 5 to 8-person team. I fostered a culture of creativity, transparency, and equity that empowered employees to improvise process improvements and bound us together as a team with common purpose. It was incredible how, as the "shy kid" just months prior, I found this ability to connect with others and make a tangible impact on their lives.

Emotional Intelligence

In college, while completing dual technical degrees, I continued my managerial and leadership training as a resident advisor. The responsibility I enjoyed most was organizing social events that exposed my residents to new activities and ideas and provided them a fun, safe environment to mingle and bond. The most impactful parts, however, were the times I mentored residents through hardship, intervened in conflicts, and managed community emergencies. Through these experiences, I discovered how to listen, how to calm, and how to lead my team and community members.

Team Player

As both a graduate student and a postdoctoral fellow, the success of my research has been dependent upon continuous collaboration with my global colleagues. I've always had a small, core team of engineers, technicians, and fellow researchers with whom I worked constantly. However, all labs have limited capabilities, so I occasionally reached out to external experts to develop partnerships, use on their equipment, and get their advice on my work. My individual projects were also part of larger research portfolios, which required that my work align with the overall strategic goals. The sum result was a complex web of people and institutions maintained by monitoring foundational relationships and dependably communicating progress.
