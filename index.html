Why You?

As I've embarked on pursuing my next career, I evaluate potential opportunities against a "corporate trifecta." The three limbs of which consist of the organizational culture and vision, the people that comprise the organization, and the clients and position responsibilities. I found that Automattic and, more specifically, WordPress VIP fulfill these requirements spectacularly, as I will expound upon below.

There are many reasons that I'm interested in joining the Automatticians, but one of the most important is that your company culture is rooted in a history of open-source and transparency. There is always something inspiring about any cause that attracts tens of thousands of people around the world to volunteer their time and expertise to the movement. I attribute much of Automattic success to its ability to make people around the world feel empowered in their work and provide them the autonomy to pursue interesting problems. The result? WordPress.org has become the most successful web design platform in the world, dramatically outpacing its commercial competitors.

Another important draw are the people themselves. As may not surprise you, both your employees and volunteers are prolific and honest in their descriptions of life as an Automattician. Much of what I have learned has come from the WordPress slack instance, community forums, and individual blogs. I've also thoroughly enjoyed plowing through Matt's podcast, Distributed, and learning about every facet of distributed work. What is immediately clear is that Automattic honors and empowers its people and the community members care deeply about each other. Who wouldn't want to be a part of that?

The final edge of the corporate trifecta consists of the clients and position responsibilities. In this case, WordPress VIP does not fail to impress. I thrive under pressure and purpose and the opportunity to collaborate with business leaders around the world on complex, interesting problems make me vibrate with excitement. It's also clear that work will never be linear or stale as every debug, expanded functionality, and site redesign is unique and brings with the opportunity to learn and grow. That combination: incredible clients, engaging problems, and continual learning, is exactly what I want in a career.

Why Me?

At first glance, my experience in software development, geology, and academic research suggests that I am primarily a scientist (not a bad thing!). While my strong technical background is undeniable, it does not define me. Instead, I've found that I am motivated by and thrive in work that requires building relationships and collaborating with others on complex problems. Automattic's combination of highly collaborative work and a globally dispersed workforce makes these skills essential tools for every employee.

As the manager of a research lab at UCSD, fresh out of highschool, I built a student worker program and hired, trained, and supervised a 5 to 8-person team. I fostered a culture of creativity, transparency, and equity that empowered employees to improvise process improvements and bound us together as a team with common purpose. It was incredible how, as the "shy kid" just months prior, I found this ability to connect with others and make a tangible impact on their lives.

In college, while completing dual technical degrees, I continued my managerial and leadership training as a resident advisor (RA). The responsibility I most enjoyed were running social events for my residents that exposed them to new activities and ideas or provided them a fun, safe environment to mingle and bond. The most impactful parts, however, were the handful of times I mentored residents through hardship, intervened in conflicts, and managed community emergencies. Regardless of the task, however, this was an opportunity rooted in and driven by working with people and was incredibly rewarding.

As an academic, in graduate school and at my current position, the success of my research has been dependent upon my continuous collaboration with colleagues around the world. I had a core team at my institution that I worked with constantly. I reached out to external experts to visit their labs, train on their equipment, and incorporate that knowledge into my work. I participated in monthly meetings with 20+ attendees to exchange ideas and constructive criticism. I presented at international conferences in front of hundreds of people to spark new and interesting conversations. The need for power skills, such as networking, relationship building, and collaboration, has become a reality of modern science

For the past 14 years, I've thrived in a diverse set of roles that have honed my interpersonal skills, provided me with extensive management experience, and made me an amiable and reliable collaborator.
